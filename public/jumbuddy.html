Overview

Jumbuddy

Jumbuddy is a hyper-local social platform that helps students discover campus events, connect through shared interests, and casually join or post activities. It's designed to help students build micro-communities by making it easy and low pressure to find a "buddy"—for anything from late-night studying to spontaneous hot pot nights.

As the UX/UI developer, I led UX research, end-to-end interface design, feature mapping, and dev handoff within a 48-hour hackathon timeline at Tufts Jumbohack.

"Networking and making friends needs to be more personable, and interactions need to extend beyond just a 'follow.'"

The Problem

Students crave connection but struggle to turn shared interests into actual interactions

Between social anxiety, clunky group chats, and busy schedules, going from "same class" to a further friendship feels like an un-scalable mountain.

User flow diagram showing core interactions

Discovery & Research

Survey

Using SurveyMonkey, we surveyed 30 college students, with the majority being in their freshman year, to gather insights:

80%
Feel like they don't have a platform to connect with other students
95%
Would love to branch out and expand their network
62%
Want to join more activities but feel fear doing so

"Networking and making friends needs to be more personable, and interactions need to extend to beyond just a 'follow.'"

Design Thinking & Methodology

Design Process

Approach

Since I was under a time constraint to hand the MVP off to the developers, I applied human-centered design principles to define and validate core user needs quickly.

Key Features

👤 Profile Creation

Students can tag their interests, add classes, and share what they're open to—no awkward cold starts

📱 Activity Feed & "Join" Flow

See who's down for anything from gym sessions to trivia nights. One tap to join, no pressure

✍️ Post an Activity

Post what you're doing or what you're looking for—friends can opt in, or strangers can become buddies

🗺️ Map Integration

See what's happening around campus in real time

Every feature was designed to make social interaction feel natural, not performative.

User Flow

To avoid assumptions and guide design decisions, I sketched a user flow that I built off of, focusing on the MVP interactions: profile setup, post creation, and event joining. Given the strict time frame, I made a conscious decision to skip wireframing and stick to a user flow in order to quickly stay aligned with the developers on feasibility.

User flow diagram - profile setup to event joining

Design Heuristics

I embedded design heuristics to ensure a smooth user experience:

  • Recognition over recall: Using icon-based tags in the activity feed area
  • Progressive disclosure: Used in the onboarding to reduce cognitive load
  • Clear affordances: Using visual hierarchy and familiar mobile patterns

While time was limited, I made sure there was space to balance speed with thoughtfulness—designing not just for screens, but for feelings, flow, and feasibility.

What I Learned

Key takeaways from building Jumbuddy

Speed requires ruthless prioritization: With only 48 hours, I learned to identify the core user flows that would validate our concept and ruthlessly cut everything else. The MVP isn't about perfection—it's about testing assumptions fast.

Design for developers, not just users: Collaborating closely with the dev team taught me that good design also considers technical constraints. Understanding MongoDB and React helped me design within realistic data structures, making handoff seamless.

Research drives empathy and prioritization: Our survey revealed that 62% of students wanted to join activities but felt fear doing so. This insight shaped our entire "low-pressure join" interaction model. Data validates intuition.

Heuristics aren't optional—they're foundational: Even under time pressure, embedding design heuristics (recognition over recall, progressive disclosure) ensured a usable MVP. Good UX principles scale across timelines.

Social features need emotional design: Building for connection isn't just about features—it's about making interactions feel natural, not performative. Every button, every flow, every word needed to reduce friction and encourage authentic engagement.
