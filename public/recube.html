Overview

Real-time data for a zero-waste supply chain

Recube partners with restaurants and malls to replace single-use packaging with a reusable container loop. As their sole UX and frontend designer, I built an analytics dashboard from scratch — giving 50+ restaurant partners live visibility into inventory, returns, and sustainability impact for the first time.

"I need to know at a glance if we're running low on containers — I don't have time to dig through reports during lunch rush."

The Problem

Partners were flying blind

The existing interface was cluttered and static — no live data, no impact metrics, no way to act quickly. Restaurant managers couldn't tell how many containers were out in the field or prove their sustainability wins to customers.

  • No real-time visibility: Inventory levels were only updated manually, causing stockouts during peak hours
  • No impact story: Partners had sustainability results but nothing to show customers or stakeholders
  • Painful search: Finding specific records meant manual exports and custom reports

The old interface — cluttered, static, no actionable data

Research & Discovery

4 interviews, 3 user types, 1 clear pattern

I talked to restaurant managers, mall operators, and Recube's internal ops team. Every group wanted the same thing: data they could act on without digging.

  • Restaurant managers needed sharable impact metrics — waste diverted, containers returned — to tell their sustainability story

  • Both groups wanted the return journey to be self-explanatory — users often didn't know where or how to return containers
The Solution

A dashboard that surfaces the right data at the right moment

I designed for two mental modes: quick-scan (is everything OK right now?) and deep-dive (show me trends over time). The layout puts live status front and center, with search and filtering that don't require any training to use.

Design Process

Research → wireframes → Figma → shipped

1. Information Architecture: Research surfaced two distinct use cases — quick operational checks and trend analysis. I separated them into a live status view and a filterable data table, so neither user had to wade through the other's workflow.

2. Wireframes: Low-fidelity wireframes let me validate hierarchy with stakeholders before any visual polish. The key decision here was what to show above the fold — we landed on inventory status and the three most-watched impact metrics.

Early wireframes defining dashboard layout and hierarchy

3. High-Fidelity in Figma: Green palette tied to Recube's brand; high-contrast text kept data legible at a glance. I built a lightweight component library so the dev handoff stayed clean — no one-off styles.

Advanced search functionality

Empty state and search prompts

4. Dev collaboration & QA: I sat in on sprint planning to flag anything technically risky before it got built. QA sessions caught edge cases — empty states, loading skeletons, error messaging — that design reviews miss.

What I Learned

Three things I'd take into any data project

Show only what drives action. My first instinct was to surface every metric we had. Stakeholders pushed back — rightly — and we cut it down to the handful of numbers that actually change what someone does next. Less noise, faster decisions.

Live data needs defensive design. Real-time dashboards break in ways static UIs don't — stale data, failed syncs, partial loads. I had to design loading states, error banners, and refresh logic as first-class screens, not afterthoughts.

Impact metrics are marketing, not just data. When restaurant managers could see "412 containers returned this month," they started putting it on their menus. Numbers become stories when framed right — that realization shaped how I wrote every label in the dashboard.
